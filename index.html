A working notebook

Fifteen calls. The opener is locked. The live read is the project.

Anthony runs clean call mechanics with the floor work locked. The discovery opener fires on fourteen of fifteen calls. The tone holds under Frank's hour-long demo through audio failures and parking-lot multitasking, under Kevin's CMO-register pressure on Cade follow-up, and under Ilya's sustained boutique-volume push-back across thirty-seven minutes. Every call ends with a clear next step. For a rep recently promoted from SDR, that is real groundwork — the holds he is starting every problem from.

The first reference call in the dataset is now on the wall: Jo-Lynn (April 29), a Canadian nurse injector in Sherwood Park. Clean approach, real discovery on lead-acquisition mechanics (Facebook as the dominant channel, $700–900/month on Google ads with low conversion, $3,000-ticket regenerative protocols where patients cannot see what they are buying), accurate read of the buying signal at the close ("this is the edge I'm maybe missing"), close run immediately, signup completed on the call, delayed start-date set cleanly. The whole boulder, sent first try.

The growth edge that keeps surfacing is foundational and it has a name: the live read. Recognizing what the prospect just said and adjusting the next move. The Ilya call (May 11) is the cleanest illustration. Ilya named the boutique-practice objection at minute seven ("we don't have the large volume of clients"), named a different expectation at minute ten ("I thought this was just a tool for us to use in the office"), repeated the dashboard-fatigue objection at minute twenty-six. The pitch lines — viral sharing, founders club, AI advisory board — ran unchanged for thirty-seven minutes. The mechanic that would have closed the call honestly was disqualification at minute thirteen, with the door left open. That mechanic has not fired yet in the dataset. It is not a missing skill. It is a missing rep.

Two more pieces from this round. The Kristin May 1 self-close is the cleanest five-minute call in the dataset — she called to sign up before the scheduled meeting; Anthony respected the signal, no re-pitch, no upsell, handled the start-date pause smoothly. Her sign-off: "thank you for calling to avoid a meeting. We don't need it." Paired with Jo-Lynn's close, that is a near-pattern now: respects the buyer's signal when they are already bought. And the closing-pitch-on-absent-decision-maker pattern formalizes with a fourth instance — Victoria's NP at 9:20 ("I don't think she's ready to make a decision") followed by the offer-extension pitch anyway. The script runs whether or not the prospect needs the script.

The next ten calls are the work, not re-reading this notebook. Three process changes in priority order: pre-demo service-toggle confirmation, trigger-driven pitch-line discipline, calibration check (pre-call when the archetype is non-standard, in-call when the prospect signals partial availability). Each worked at the next-call cadence, not by absorbing the doc.

Below: trend metrics across the dataset, a five-skill assessment of where each discipline is landing, a field guide to the five sections of the climb the per-deal scorecard grades against, a coaching field kit drawn from the actual moments, and the fifteen calls in chronological order — the reference send (Jo-Lynn), the canonical illustration (Maria), the boutique no-fit (Ilya), the masthead chaos call (Frank), the sharpest closing-on-absent-DM (Victoria's NP), the register mismatch with a sophisticated buyer (Cade and Kevin, original and follow-up), and the rest where the same root pattern surfaces in different shapes. The cat, of course, watches from the rock.
